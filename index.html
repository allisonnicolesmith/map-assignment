This year has been one of the deadliest years on record for hate violence against transgender and gender-nonconforming people in the United States.

Content warning: This article includes details of anti-trans hate violence.

At least 41 transgender and gender nonconforming people have been killed this year, more than any other year on record.

Geography appears to play a role. Of the victims, 50% were killed in the Southern U.S., while 26% of the murders took place in the Midwest, Northeast and West combined.

“Violence stemming from a ‘culture of honor’ is rampant throughout the region,” said Dov Cohen, a cultural psychologist at the University of Illinois.

In 1996, Cohen conducted an experiment to test if Southerners were more prone to violence.

Subjects underwent a series of tests designed to challenge their masculinity: forced games of “chicken,” people bumping into them and colorful name-calling.

Southerners were not only visibly angry, they were angrier on chemical level, showing significantly levels of higher cortisol.

“Factor in religion and poverty, it’s not surprising hate violence of this kind is more prevalent in the South,” said Cohen.
